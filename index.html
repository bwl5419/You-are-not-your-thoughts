You're not in crisis. You're just tired of the same thought showing up every night.

The internet sends you to therapy or a breathing app — but sometimes you don't need either. You just need a minute to notice the loop, name it, and step a half-step outside of it.

How your mind works

Your brain runs a few tricks on you, and they all feel like truth.

Psychologists call these cognitive distortions. We'll call them what they are: shortcuts your brain takes that feel real but aren't.

01 — all or nothing

"I bombed that one question, so the whole interview was a disaster."

Your brain skips the middle. One rough moment becomes the whole story. Real life almost always lives in the gray.

02 — mind reading

"She didn't text back. She must be mad at me."

You're guessing what someone else is thinking — and your guess is usually the worst one. Ask yourself: what else could be true?

03 — fortune telling

"I'll definitely fail this midterm. Why even study."

You're certain the future is bad, so you stop trying. But certainty is a feeling, not a fact. You haven't taken the test yet.

First steps

Small prompts. Five minutes. No streaks to break.

When the loop starts again, pick one. Answer in a notes app, on paper, or in your head on the walk home. There's no right way.

~5 minutes start anywhere
prompt 03 reframe

What's a version of this thought that's 10% truer?

Not toxic positivity. Just a little more accurate. "I'm failing" becomes "I'm behind in one class and I can catch up."

prompt 02 question

If a friend said this about themselves, what would you tell them?

You're almost always kinder to other people than to yourself. Borrow some of that kindness.

prompt 01 notice

What's one thought you've had three times today?

Write it down exactly as it shows up, harsh words and all. Seeing it on the page makes it smaller.
